---
title: Is Your LTV Figure Doing More Harm Than Good
date: "2026-04-15"
slug: ltv-distribution-not-average
---

The average LTV figure in your dashboard is doing more harm than good.

It flattens every cohort into a single number. The number looks about right. It just doesn't tell you whether that's a full-price cohort or a sample sale cohort, a February with no activity or an August with three promotions running.

A single blended figure hides low-quality cohorts, masks channel-specific inefficiencies, and makes unsustainable acquisition costs look fine on paper.

Shopify's own guide to calculating customer lifetime value teaches the standard formula: average order value, purchase frequency, customer lifespan. It's not wrong. It's just a point estimate. It doesn't distinguish between different behavioural profiles sitting inside that number. The calculation is the easy part. The problem is what you lose when you aggregate.

Peter Fader, Wharton's leading researcher on customer valuation, makes the point directly: don't tell me what the average LTV is. Tell me what the distribution is. Some customers will go through the gates of hell to stay with you. Most are indifferent. A blended average tells you nothing about which is which or how many of the former you actually have.

Knowing your LTV distribution by cohort, by channel and acquisition condition is what customer centricity actually looks like in practice. Not a single number on a dashboard. A clear view of which customers are worth more, why and what you can project with confidence from that.

A projection you can't interrogate is just a guess with better formatting.
