Your best acquisition product probably isn't your bestseller.

It's whichever product produces the highest repeat rate, adjusted for volume.

The default is to optimise order one for margin or AOV. Neither tells you whether that customer comes back.

A product with 40% repeat rate from 400 customers beats one with 60% from 12. The volume matters. A small sample at a high rate is noise. Scale changes the signal. Most reporting tools show you the rate. Not whether you can trust it.

The result is acquisition decisions built on a number that looks like a fact and isn't.

In fashion, a sale item brings someone in at a price point that was never sustainable. The repeat rate on that cohort looks fine until you check what they paid on order two. Or whether there was an order two.

In wellness, a customer who comes in through a hero product with strong reviews behaves differently to one who came in through a bundle deal or a gift set. Same category. Different customer. Few operators ever separate them.

In luxury, volume is low enough that a handful of high-repeat customers can distort the whole picture. A 70% repeat rate from 8 customers is three people who really like you. That is not a signal you can acquire against.

You end up spending to bring in customers who looked like your best cohort on paper and turned out to be your worst. The data was there. It just wasn't adjusted for anything.

This connects directly to what I wrote earlier this week about LTV. A blended average hides which cohorts are worth acquiring. The first purchase is where that starts. Get the entry product wrong and the LTV problem is baked in before you've noticed it.
