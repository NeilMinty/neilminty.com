title: Retention, Measured Properly
date: "2026-04-10"

---

Retention is the most cited priority in DTC right now. It's also the most inconsistently measured.

Most brands track churn. Fewer track churn by cohort. Fewer still track the early behavioural signals that predict churn three or four weeks before it shows up in the numbers.

The metrics that actually matter:

**Subscription churn rate** — the headline figure, but only useful when segmented. Overall churn of 6% can mask a cohort acquired through a specific promotion churning at 18%. The blended number hides the problem.

**Cohort LTV** — not projected, actual. What did customers acquired in a given month go on to spend over 90, 180, 365 days? This is the number that tells you whether your acquisition economics are working, not CAC in isolation.

**Win-back rate** — the percentage of lapsed customers who reactivate. According to Omniconvert, CAC has risen 233% since 2015. A reactivated customer costs a fraction of a new one. Most brands have no systematic view of what their win-back rate actually is.

**Repeat purchase rate by first SKU** — which products create long-term customers and which create one-time buyers? According to industry benchmarks, 60% of DTC brand revenue comes from returning customers. The first product someone buys is one of the strongest predictors of whether they become one — though [repeat rate signals are only reliable once you have sufficient volume behind them](https://firstpurchasepredictor.com).

**Days between purchases** — when this starts extending across a cohort, something has changed. Price, product, experience, competitive pressure. The metric surfaces the signal before the churn does.

The problem isn't that brands don't know these metrics exist. It's that pulling them together requires stitching ecommerce, email, loyalty and your subscription platform into a view that nobody has time to build manually every week.

By the time retention becomes the agenda item, the window to intervene has usually passed.
