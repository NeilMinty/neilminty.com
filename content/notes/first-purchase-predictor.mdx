Which product a customer buys first predicts whether they come back.

Most DTC brands know this intuitively. Very few act on it in any structured way because the data is spread across your ESP, your analytics platform, a Loop Returns export someone did six months ago and a spreadsheet that lives on one person's laptop.

So the default is to look at repeat rate as a single number and move on. Which is fine until you notice that your highest volume entry product and your best retention product are different products. Which they usually are. Then you have a problem, because your acquisition spend is probably optimised toward the wrong one.

I've been in a few conversations recently about this — one about why days between purchases start stretching before churn ever shows up in your dashboard, another about why LTV as a single number is almost meaningless without the signals underneath it. Both landed with people who are clearly thinking about this seriously. Which suggested it was worth building something rather than just talking about it.

There are a few things that matter more than the headline repeat rate and almost never get looked at in the same place:

Whether the cohort was acquired at full price. A 60% repeat rate built on promotional buyers is a different signal to 60% built on full-price customers. It may not hold when you start spending properly against it.

Whether spend is still compounding after 90 days. A product that drives strong 90-day repeat but flatlines at 180 has a ceiling most teams don't see until it's already too late to do much about it.

Volume confidence. Four customers at 80% repeat is not the same as 400 customers at 80% repeat. Treating them identically in a weekly trade meeting is how you make acquisition decisions that look right and aren't.
